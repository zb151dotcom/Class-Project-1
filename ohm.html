Artificial Intelligence and Privacy

Group: Group 1 / Riley Bone, John Bozell, Shalonte Branham, Zachary Broyles
Date: February/17/2026
Topic: Fourth Amendment and AI Surveillance

Structured Summary: Ohm (2012) as a Counterpoint to Solove

Paul Ohm, The Fourth Amendment in a World Without Privacy, 81 MISS. L.J. 1309 (2012), https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2073574 [https://perma.cc/WJG9-Q24Y].

Central thesis. Ohm argues that as the private sector builds a pervasive “surveillance society,” Fourth Amendment doctrine that uses “privacy” as its proxy will steadily contract. When people routinely disclose data to devices, platforms, and cloud intermediaries, courts applying expectation-of-privacy and “assumption of risk” logic will increasingly treat government access to those records as non-search activity. Preserving meaningful limits therefore requires more than “mild tweaks” to the third-party doctrine; it requires a “third act” that reorients Fourth Amendment analysis toward constraining state power despite declining baseline privacy. (Ohm, supra, at 1310–12)

1) Problem framing: privatized surveillance as a constitutional stress test. The Introduction frames a two-step dynamic: (i) businesses capture and store granular behavioral data at scale, and (ii) law enforcement later obtains those records more easily than it could have collected directly. The constitutional risk is structural: the state can become a downstream consumer of private surveillance, while doctrine built around “reasonable expectations” treats widespread disclosure as forfeiture of protection. (Id. at 1310–12.)

2) Part I — “The Death of Privacy”: why private surveillance becomes ubiquitous. Ohm identifies four reinforcing drivers: “the one device” (smartphones as always-on sensors), “the cloud” (cheap storage/processing), “the social” (normalized sharing and network effects), and “Big Data” (analytics that extract value from scale). These forces yield a “surveillance society,” where collection is continuous, secondary use is routine, and opting out is increasingly unrealistic. (Id. at 1314–20.)

3) Part II — “The Diminished Fourth Amendment”: why privacy-based doctrine fails. Part II argues that privacy-centered Fourth Amendment doctrine—especially under Katz—can become self-defeating when privacy is already collapsing. Ohm critiques “self-help” assumptions as unrealistic against pervasive tracking and describes the “no more expectations of privacy” trap: when ubiquitous disclosure is treated as “assumption of risk” or “knowing exposure,” diminished social privacy becomes the doctrinal reason to diminish constitutional protection. He urges a pivot “from privacy to power”—treating the Fourth Amendment as a structural check on state coercion and informational dominance, not merely a mirror of prevailing privacy norms. (Id. at 1320–36; see also Katz v. United States, 389 U.S. 347 (1967).)

4) Part III — “The Fourth Amendment’s Third Act”: what comes after Katz. Ohm suggests Katz is becoming “the new Olmstead” and develops “equilibrium adjustment” as a method: courts should preserve the constitutional balance between police power and citizen liberty as investigative capabilities shift. He argues for objective metrics to discipline when adjustment is warranted. (Ohm, supra, at 1336–47; see Olmstead v. United States, 277 U.S. 438 (1928); cf. Orin S. Kerr, An Equilibrium-Adjustment Theory of the Fourth Amendment, 125 HARV. L. REV. 476 (2011).)

5) Conclusion: what survives when “privacy” no longer does. Ohm concludes that if Fourth Amendment protection is tethered only to expectations of privacy, pervasive private surveillance will predictably generate weaker limits on government access to personal data. The durable project, on his account, is maintaining meaningful constitutional constraints on state power in a society where private actors routinely collect what the government wants. (Ohm, supra, at 1353–56.)
